**Whales in the Ocean**

Whales are among the largest animals on Earth. They live in the ocean but breathe air like us, and they are more closely related to dolphins than to fish. [1] In fact, whales are mammals—they give birth to live young, feed them with milk, and have to surface regularly to breathe. From the huge blue whale, which can grow longer than a bus, to the smaller killer whale that hunts in groups, whales have fascinated humans for centuries. Today, we understand more about their behaviour, but they still hold many secrets. This article explores the lives of these remarkable creatures.

Whales have evolved to live in the sea. [2] Their bodies are streamlined for swimming—smooth, without much hair, and shaped to move through water with less effort. They have a thick layer of fat called blubber（鲸脂）to keep warm in cold oceans. Instead of legs, they have flippers（鳍状肢）and a powerful tail fluke that pushes them forward. Most whales have excellent hearing; they use sounds to communicate, find food, and navigate in the dark depths. Some species can dive hundreds of metres and hold their breath for over an hour. Every part of their body is adapted for life in the water.

Whales travel long distances every year. [3] Many species migrate from cold feeding grounds in summer to warmer breeding waters in winter. A humpback whale may swim thousands of kilometres between its feeding and breeding areas. Mothers travel with their calves, teaching them the routes passed down through generations. Scientists are still learning how whales find their way—they may use the Earth's magnetic field, the sun, or memory of coastline shapes. Migration is exhausting and dangerous, but it is essential for finding food and raising young. These journeys connect different parts of the ocean ecosystem.

Whales face serious threats from human activity. [4] In the past, whaling（捕鲸）reduced many populations to near extinction. Today, whaling is limited by international law, but other dangers remain. Ship strikes can injure or kill whales. Underwater noise from ships and oil drilling can disturb their communication and navigation. Plastic and other trash in the ocean can be eaten by mistake or entangle whales. Climate change is altering ocean temperatures and food supplies. Many countries and organisations now work to protect whales through sanctuaries, speed limits for ships, and rules against pollution. Conservation efforts are slowly helping some populations recover.

[5] Whales connect us to the ocean's past and remind us of our responsibility to protect it. They have survived for millions of years, but their future depends on the choices we make today. By learning about whales, supporting conservation, and reducing ocean pollution, we can help ensure that these magnificent animals continue to swim in our seas for generations to come. The next time you think of the ocean, remember the whales—and the fragile world they call home.
