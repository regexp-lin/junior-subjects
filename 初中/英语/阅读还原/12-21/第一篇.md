# 12-21 第一篇

**Light Pollution: When Night Loses Its Darkness**

When you look up at the night sky from a city, how many stars can you see? For most city dwellers, the answer is very few. Street lamps, billboards, and building lights glow around the clock, washing out the darkness. This excess of artificial light is called light pollution. It affects not only our view of the stars but also wildlife, human health, and the environment. [1]

Many animals depend on natural patterns of light and dark. Birds that migrate at night can become confused by bright city lights and crash into buildings. Sea turtle hatchlings, which normally find the ocean by moving toward the lighter horizon, may head toward inland lights instead and never reach the water. Insects swarm around street lamps and die, disrupting food chains. Even plants can be affected—some trees near constant lights keep their leaves longer, which can harm them in winter. [2]

Humans are also impacted. Our bodies have internal clocks that rely on darkness to produce melatonin, a hormone that helps us sleep. Too much artificial light at night can disrupt this rhythm, leading to sleep problems, fatigue, and even a higher risk of some health conditions. People who work night shifts or live in brightly lit areas often report worse sleep quality. [3]

Solutions exist. Cities can use "dark sky" friendly lighting—shielded lamps that point downward instead of upward, warmer-colored lights that are less disruptive, and timers that turn lights off when they are not needed. Some communities have created dark sky reserves where people can still enjoy a natural starry night. Individuals can also help by turning off unnecessary lights at home and choosing outdoor lights that do not glare into the sky. [4]

Reducing light pollution does not mean living in darkness. It means using light wisely—where we need it, when we need it, and in ways that do not harm the night. [5]
