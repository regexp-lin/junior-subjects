**Movie Special Effects: How Magic Happens on Screen**

When you watch a movie, you often see things that seem impossible—dragons flying, cities being destroyed, or actors travelling through time. How do filmmakers create these scenes? [1] In fact, they use special effects, or "SFX," to make the impossible look real. Special effects have changed a lot over the years. In the past, filmmakers used models, make-up, and clever camera tricks. Today, computers play a huge role. That is why modern movies can show worlds that exist only in the imagination. From small indie films to big blockbusters（大片）, special effects help tell stories that would otherwise be impossible to film.

What are the main types of special effects? [2] Practical effects are done during filming—explosions, stunts（特技）, and make-up. Computer-generated imagery, or CGI, is created on computers after filming. Motion capture records actors' movements and turns them into digital characters. Green screens let filmmakers place actors in any background. In other words, filmmakers often combine different techniques to get the best result. A single scene might use real explosions, CGI creatures, and digital backgrounds—all working together.

How has technology changed special effects? [3] In the 1970s and 80s, films like Star Wars used physical models and miniatures（微缩模型）. By the 1990s, computers began to create dinosaurs, spaceships, and fantasy worlds. Today, software can create almost anything—realistic water, fire, crowds, and entire cities. For this reason, the line between "real" and "computer-made" has become very thin. Audiences often cannot tell what was filmed and what was created on a computer. The goal is always the same: to make the audience believe.

Do special effects replace real acting? [4] No—the best films use effects to support the story, not to replace it. Actors still need to perform with emotion and belief. Even when surrounded by green screens and imaginary creatures, they must make the audience care. It is true that technology has made many things possible. But a great film still needs a great story and great performances. Special effects are tools; they serve the story. When used well, they make movies more powerful, not less human.

[5] In short, special effects have opened up new possibilities for storytelling. They let filmmakers bring dreams to life and take audiences to places they could never go in real life. Whether you prefer practical stunts or digital magic, the aim is always to create wonder and emotion. Remember: the best special effects are the ones you do not notice—they feel so real that you forget they are effects at all. So next time you watch a movie, look closely—you might be surprised by how much "magic" is on the screen.
