**Polar Bears: Giants of the Arctic**

Polar bears are the largest land carnivores in the world. They live in the Arctic, where snow and ice cover the landscape for most of the year. Their thick white fur helps them blend in with the snow and stay warm in freezing temperatures. Under their fur, they have black skin that absorbs heat from the sun. These adaptations make them perfectly suited for life in the cold. [1]

Polar bears hunt mainly seals, which provide the fat and protein they need to survive. They wait at breathing holes in the ice or swim silently toward their prey. A single seal can feed a bear for days. In summer, when the ice melts, some bears go without food for long periods. They rely on stored fat to get through the lean season. [2]

Climate change is the biggest threat to polar bears. Sea ice is melting earlier and forming later each year. Bears need the ice to hunt seals and to travel between areas. With less ice, they have to swim farther and spend more energy. Some bears have starved or drowned when they could not find enough food or resting spots. [3]

Scientists and governments are working to protect polar bears. Some areas are set aside as reserves where hunting is limited or banned. Research helps us understand how bears are affected by changes in their habitat. Reducing carbon emissions can slow the loss of sea ice and give bears a better chance to survive. [4]

Polar bears are a symbol of the Arctic and of the need to act on climate change. Their fate is linked to the health of the ice and the ocean. By protecting polar bears, we also protect the entire Arctic ecosystem and the planet we share. [5]
