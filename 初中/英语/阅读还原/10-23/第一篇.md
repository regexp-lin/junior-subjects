**The Educational Value of Housework**

Many parents wonder whether they should ask their children to help with chores. Some worry that chores will take time away from homework or hobbies. But research and experience suggest that household tasks can teach children important life skills. [1] Doing chores is not just about keeping the house tidy—it is a way for children to learn responsibility, independence, and the value of contributing to the family.

Young children can start with simple tasks. They might put away their toys, set the table, or help fold laundry. As they grow older, they can take on more—washing dishes, taking out the trash, or cooking simple meals. [2] Each task, no matter how small, helps children build habits and confidence, so that by the time they leave home, they are ready to manage their own lives.

Chores also teach time management. When children have both homework and chores, they learn to plan their day and prioritize. They discover that work comes before play and that everyone in the family has a role. [3] These lessons extend beyond the home—they prepare young people for school, jobs, and relationships, where responsibility and teamwork matter.

Some parents hesitate to assign chores because they want to protect their children from stress. Yet studies show that children who do chores tend to have higher self-esteem and better social skills. They feel capable and useful. [4] Giving children age-appropriate chores does not burden them—it empowers them and helps them grow into capable, responsible adults.

Of course, chores should match a child's age and abilities. A five-year-old can water plants; a teenager can help with grocery shopping. The goal is not to overwhelm but to gradually build skills. [5] When parents include their children in household work in a positive way, they give them a gift that lasts a lifetime—the ability to take care of themselves and others.
