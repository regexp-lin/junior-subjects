**The Wonder of Animal Migration**

Every year, billions of animals—birds, fish, mammals, and insects—travel thousands of miles across the earth. They fly over oceans, swim through rivers, and walk across continents. This amazing behavior is called migration. Scientists have studied it for decades, yet many mysteries remain. [1] Migration reveals both the toughness of life and the delicate balance of nature. Understanding it helps us protect these incredible travelers and the ecosystems they depend on.

Why do animals migrate? [2] The main reasons are food, weather, and breeding. In winter, many birds fly south to find warmer weather and more food. Salmon swim upstream to lay their eggs in the rivers where they were born. Wildebeests in Africa follow the rains to reach fresh grasslands. Each species has developed its own migration pattern over millions of years. These journeys are essential for survival—without them, many populations would not last.

Migration is full of dangers. [3] Migrating animals face predators, bad weather, and human-made obstacles. Birds can be exhausted by long flights and sometimes crash into buildings or power lines. Fish must pass dams and polluted waters. Some animals cross busy roads and risk being hit by cars. Habitat loss along migration routes makes the journey even harder. Conservation groups work to create safe passages and protect critical stopping points. The challenges are real, but so is the animals' determination.

How do they find their way? [4] Different animals use different methods. Birds may use the sun, stars, Earth's magnetic field, or landmarks to navigate. Salmon are thought to use the smell of their home river. Monarch butterflies, which travel from Canada to Mexico, are born with a built-in sense of direction. Scientists are still learning how these navigation systems work. What is clear is that migration requires remarkable skills that we are only beginning to understand.

Animal migration is one of nature's greatest shows. [5] By protecting migration routes and reducing threats, we can ensure that future generations will witness these extraordinary journeys. Every butterfly, bird, and whale that completes its migration is a triumph of life. Let us do our part to keep these ancient paths open. The survival of countless species—and the health of our planet—depends on it.
