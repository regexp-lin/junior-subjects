**Noise Pollution: The Invisible Threat to Our Health**

When we think about pollution, we often picture dirty air, polluted water, or plastic waste. But there is another kind of pollution that affects millions of people every day: noise. Traffic, construction, loud music, and even noisy neighbours can make our surroundings unpleasant—and sometimes harmful. [1] In fact, constant exposure to loud noise can damage your hearing over time. It can also cause stress, sleep problems, and difficulty concentrating. That is why noise is more than just an annoyance—it is a real health risk. The World Health Organisation says that noise pollution is one of the top environmental threats to public health in Europe, and the problem is growing in cities worldwide.

Where does noise pollution come from? [2] Road traffic is the biggest source in most cities—cars, buses, and trucks create a steady hum. Construction sites, factories, and airports add to the problem. In homes, loud music, televisions, and household appliances（家用电器）can reach harmful levels. Even schools and hospitals can be noisy. In other words, noise surrounds us in almost every part of modern life. It is hard to escape, especially in crowded cities where space is limited and many people live close together.

What can we do to reduce noise? [3] Governments can set noise limits for roads, construction sites, and factories. They can build noise barriers（隔音屏障）along highways and create quiet zones in parks. Individuals can wear earplugs in loud places, use headphones responsibly, and avoid making unnecessary noise late at night. For this reason, fighting noise pollution needs both public action and personal responsibility. Simple steps—like closing windows facing a busy street or choosing quieter appliances—can make a difference at home.

Why is quiet important for health? [4] Our ears need rest from constant sound. Too much noise can raise stress hormones, increase blood pressure, and disturb sleep. Children may find it harder to learn in noisy classrooms. It is true that we often underestimate the effect of noise on our well-being. Studies show that people who live near busy roads or airports report more health problems. Finding quiet time each day—even a few minutes—can help our minds and bodies recover. So protecting our ears and seeking quiet spaces is part of taking care of ourselves.

[5] In short, noise pollution is a serious issue that we need to take seriously. It affects our hearing, our stress levels, and our quality of life. Whether we live in a busy city or a quiet village, we can all take steps to reduce noise and protect our health. Remember: your ears cannot "close"—they are always listening. So the next time you turn up the volume or hear construction outside, think about the impact. A quieter environment is a healthier one, for everyone.
