# 10-08 第二篇

**Melting Glaciers and a Changing World**

Glaciers—large masses of ice that move slowly over land—store most of the world's freshwater. They provide water for rivers, farms, and cities, especially in dry seasons. Yet glaciers are melting faster than ever due to rising global temperatures. Scientists have observed this trend for decades, and it is accelerating. [1] The consequences reach far beyond the mountains where glaciers are found, touching nearly every continent and affecting both coastal and inland communities.

When glaciers melt, they release huge amounts of water into rivers and lakes. In the short term, this can cause flooding, especially when ice dams burst. Communities living below glaciers have experienced deadly floods as meltwater suddenly rushes down. [2] Scientists measure the thickness and area of glaciers each year to track how quickly they are disappearing and to predict when rivers that depend on them may run low.

Rising sea levels are also linked to glacier melt. Ice stored on land in Greenland and Antarctica, when it flows into the ocean, raises sea levels worldwide. Coastal cities and island nations face growing risks of erosion and flooding. [3] The good news is that if we act quickly to cut emissions, we can slow the rate of melt and give communities more time to adapt to the changes that are already underway.

Many countries are taking action. Some are building reservoirs to store meltwater. Others are developing early warning systems for glacier-related floods. International efforts to cut carbon emissions aim to limit further warming. [4] No single country can solve this problem alone, and the poorest nations—those that have contributed least to climate change—often face the greatest risks from glacier loss.

The fate of the world's glaciers depends on the choices we make now. If we continue to burn fossil fuels at current rates, many glaciers could disappear within this century. [5] What we do in the next few decades will determine how much ice remains and how prepared we are to face a world with less glacial water and higher seas.

## 选项

A. Glacier melt is not only a sign of climate change—it threatens water supplies, increases the risk of floods, and affects the lives of millions of people who depend on glacial water for drinking and farming.
B. Over time, as glaciers shrink, the water supply becomes unreliable—rivers may run high in spring and early summer but dry up later in the year when the ice is gone.
C. Slowing glacier melt requires reducing greenhouse gas emissions globally, as there is no quick fix once the ice begins to disappear at this scale.
D. Each of these steps—from local adaptation to global cooperation—will be needed if we are to manage the impact of melting glaciers and protect those most at risk.
E. The ice that has taken thousands of years to form may vanish in decades, but it is not too late to slow this loss and to adapt to the changes that can no longer be avoided.
F. For example, the Himalayan glaciers supply water to over a billion people in Asia, and their retreat could affect the region's food security and stability.
