**Glaciers and Global Warming**

Glaciers are huge masses of ice that form in cold mountain areas or near the poles. They move slowly downhill, carving valleys and shaping the land over thousands of years. Glaciers store about three-quarters of the world's fresh water and are sometimes called "water towers" because they provide water for rivers and lakes in many regions. [1] In other words, glaciers are vital for both the landscape and the water supply of millions of people. Yet today, glaciers are melting at a speed that worries scientists around the world.

The main cause of glacier melt is rising temperatures. [2] As the Earth warms because of greenhouse gases—such as carbon dioxide from burning coal and petrol—snow and ice melt faster than they can be replaced. In summer, glaciers naturally lose some ice, but in winter new snow usually adds it back. When winters become warmer and shorter, and summers become hotter, the balance is broken. More ice melts than forms each year, so glaciers shrink. Scientists measure this change using satellites and on-the-ground studies to track how fast glaciers are retreating.

Melting glaciers bring serious problems. [3] First, they can cause floods when meltwater suddenly fills rivers or when ice blocks give way. Second, as glaciers shrink, many rivers that rely on them will have less water in the dry season, which can affect farming and drinking water for millions. Third, when glaciers melt into the ocean, they add to sea-level rise, putting coastal cities at risk. These effects touch people far from the mountains—anyone who depends on rivers fed by glaciers or lives near the sea may feel the impact.

What can we do? [4] Reducing greenhouse gas emissions is the most important step. This means using less fossil fuel, switching to renewable energy like wind and solar, and making our cars, buildings, and factories more efficient. Governments, businesses, and individuals all have a role to play. Some countries have set targets to cut emissions and protect glacier regions. While we cannot stop melting overnight, taking action now can slow it down and help communities adapt to changes that are already happening.

[5] Glaciers are a precious resource and a sign of the health of our planet. By understanding how they are changing and why, we can take responsibility for our impact on the Earth. The choices we make today about energy and the environment will shape the future of glaciers—and the millions of people who depend on them.
