**The Value of Botanical Gardens**

Botanical gardens are more than pretty places to walk. They are living museums of plants—scientific, educational, and beautiful. [1] In short, they collect, grow, and display plants from around the world for research, teaching, and the public to enjoy. From tiny mosses（苔藓）to tall trees, from common flowers to rare species that are almost extinct, botanical gardens protect and showcase the richness of plant life. They have existed for hundreds of years, and today they play an even greater role in a world facing climate change and habitat loss.

Botanical gardens support science and conservation. [2] Scientists study plants in gardens to understand how they grow, adapt, and interact with other living things. Seeds from rare or endangered plants are often stored in seed banks so that future generations can restore them if they disappear in the wild. Many gardens work with universities and research centres to develop new medicines from plants or to find crops that can grow in dry or salty soils. By sharing data and specimens（标本）with each other, gardens around the world help protect plant diversity. This work is vital for food security and for saving species.

Education is at the heart of botanical gardens. [3] Schools bring students on visits to learn about plant life cycles, ecosystems, and the importance of protecting nature. Gardens offer workshops, guided tours, and signs that explain each plant's name, origin, and uses. Children can touch leaves, smell flowers, and see insects that depend on plants—experiences that books and screens cannot replace. For many people, a first visit to a botanical garden sparks a lifelong interest in plants and the environment. Learning in a real, living setting makes knowledge stick.

Gardens also provide space for relaxation and health. [4] Spending time among trees and flowers can reduce stress, lower blood pressure, and improve mood. In busy cities, botanical gardens are quiet places where people can escape noise and traffic. Families picnic on the grass; artists draw and paint; visitors simply stroll and breathe. Some gardens even offer "forest bathing"（森林浴）—the practice of walking slowly in a woodland to relax the mind. Whether for science, learning, or rest, botanical gardens serve the whole community.

[5] Botanical gardens remind us that plants are the base of life—they give us food, air, and beauty. As cities grow and nature shrinks, these gardens become more important than ever. The next time you pass a botanical garden, consider stepping in. You might discover a new favourite flower, learn something surprising, or simply enjoy a peaceful hour among the green. A visit is a small step toward appreciating and protecting the natural world.
