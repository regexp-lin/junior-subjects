**Earthquake Safety: How to Stay Safe When the Ground Shakes**

Earthquakes happen when two sections of Earth's crust suddenly move past each other. They can occur almost anywhere, though some regions—such as the Pacific "Ring of Fire"—experience them more often. Earthquakes cannot be predicted exactly, but scientists can identify areas that are at higher risk. When an earthquake strikes, the ground may shake for only a few seconds or for more than a minute. The shaking can cause buildings to collapse, roads to crack, and landslides to occur. [1] Understanding how earthquakes work and how to prepare for them can greatly reduce the danger and save lives when the ground begins to move.

The first rule during an earthquake is to protect yourself immediately. If you are indoors, "Drop, Cover, and Hold On" is the recommended approach. Drop to your hands and knees, take cover under a strong desk or table, and hold on until the shaking stops. Stay away from windows, heavy furniture, and objects that might fall. If you are outdoors, move to an open area away from buildings, trees, and power lines. [2] These simple steps, practiced in advance, can make the difference between minor injuries and serious harm when an earthquake occurs.

After the shaking stops, the danger is not over. Aftershocks—smaller earthquakes that follow the main one—are common and can cause further damage. Check yourself and others for injuries. Be careful of broken glass, fallen objects, and gas leaks. If you are in a damaged building, leave calmly and go to a safe outdoor area. [3] In the moments following an earthquake, staying calm and following a clear plan helps you avoid additional risks and assist those who need help.

Before an earthquake, there is a lot you can do to prepare. Have an emergency kit with water, food, a flashlight, a first-aid kit, and important documents. Make a family plan so everyone knows where to meet and how to contact each other if separated. Secure heavy items like bookshelves and TVs to walls so they do not fall during shaking. [4] Good preparation before an earthquake can reduce fear and confusion when it happens, making it easier to respond quickly and correctly.

Earthquakes are a natural part of Earth's activity, and we cannot prevent them. What we can do is learn about them, prepare our homes and families, and know how to react when they occur. Schools and communities often hold earthquake drills to practice the right actions. [5] By taking earthquake safety seriously, we can protect ourselves and our loved ones even when the unexpected happens.
