# 08-11 第一篇

**Urban Agriculture: Growing Food in the City**

As cities grow, so does the need for fresh, local food. Urban agriculture—growing crops or raising animals in cities—is becoming more popular around the world. Rooftop gardens, community plots, and indoor farms are turning concrete spaces into green ones. [1] This movement is not just about food; it connects people to nature, strengthens communities, and can even improve mental health. Understanding its full value helps explain why more cities are supporting it.

One clear benefit of urban farming is reduced "food miles." [2] When vegetables are grown on a rooftop or in a neighborhood garden, they do not need to travel far to reach your plate. This means fewer trucks on the road, less pollution, and fresher produce. City dwellers who grow their own herbs or tomatoes often report that the taste is noticeably better than store-bought alternatives. Local food is often healthier and more sustainable.

Urban farms also create green spaces in crowded areas. [3] Vacant lots and rooftops that were once unused can become places where people grow food, relax, and meet neighbors. Studies show that access to greenery reduces stress and improves well-being. School gardens teach children where food comes from and how to care for plants. These spaces bring both environmental and social benefits.

Urban agriculture can also create jobs and build skills. [4] From selling produce at farmers' markets to managing vertical farms, there are growing opportunities in this field. Some programs train unemployed youth in farming and business skills, giving them a path to employment. As technology advances, urban farming may become an even bigger part of the economy in many cities.

[5] Urban agriculture faces challenges such as limited space and pollution from traffic, but creative solutions continue to emerge. Rooftop soil can be tested for safety; hydroponics and vertical farming use less land. With support from governments and communities, urban farming can help cities become greener, healthier, and more self-sufficient in the years to come.

## 选项

A. The wider impact goes beyond what we put on our tables.

## 答案键（Answer Key）

[1] A   [2] B   [3] C   [4] D   [5] E

## 深度解析

- **[1] A**：
  - **线索**：空前介绍城市农业的定义及各种形式（屋顶、社区、室内），空后列举food之外的多重价值：连接自然、强化社区、改善心理健康。A项"更广泛的影响超出我们餐桌上的内容"承上（not just about food）启下（connects、strengthens、improves），概括衔接。

- **[2] B**：
  - **线索**：本段主题为减少"食物里程"，写本地种植减少运输、污染、更新鲜。B项"从农田到餐桌的距离更短，对人和地球都有益"与reduced food miles、fewer trucks、fresher produce逻辑一致。

- **[3] C**：
  - **线索**：段中写空地、屋顶变为种植与社交场所，绿化减压、学校菜园育人。C项"空置的城市空间可转化为有价值的社区资产"概括本段内容。

- **[4] D**：
  - **线索**：段首点明创造就业与技能，后文写农贸市场、垂直农场、青年培训项目。D项"这一增长中的产业为工作与学习提供新机遇"与段意相符。

- **[5] E**：
  - **线索**：结尾段以[5]开头，后文写面临空间、污染等挑战，但创意方案不断涌现，展望未来。E项"尽管存在障碍，城市农业前景光明"总领全段，点明挑战与希望并存。

- **干扰项分析**：
  - **F. For example...**：以For example开头，新加坡垂直农场为单一案例，与任一段落的概括或承启功能不符，论述层次错位。
