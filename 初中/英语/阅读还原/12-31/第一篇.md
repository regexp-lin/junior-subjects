# 12-31 第一篇

**New Year Traditions Around the World**

People everywhere celebrate the start of a new year. Different cultures have their own customs—some shared, some unique. In many places, midnight on 31 December marks the turning point. Fireworks light up the sky, and people count down together. The new year is seen as a chance to leave the past behind and start fresh. [1]

Food often plays a special role. In Spain, people eat 12 grapes at midnight—one for each chime of the clock—to bring luck for each month. In the southern United States, black-eyed peas and greens are eaten for prosperity. In China, dumplings at Lunar New Year symbolise wealth and family unity. These foods link celebration with hopes for the year ahead. [2]

Many people make New Year resolutions. They promise to exercise more, study harder, or give up a bad habit. Resolutions show our desire to improve, even if we do not always keep them. Writing down goals and sharing them with others can make success more likely. The new year offers a natural moment to reflect and plan. [3]

Cleaning and decorating are common. In Japan, people clean their homes before the new year to welcome good fortune. In Scotland, "first-footing" means the first person to enter a home after midnight should bring gifts like coal or whisky. These rituals create a sense of order and hope as the calendar turns. [4]

New Year traditions connect us to our culture and to each other. They remind us that time moves forward and that we can choose how to use it. Whether we stay up until midnight, share a meal, or set new goals, we join millions around the world in welcoming another year of possibilities. [5]
