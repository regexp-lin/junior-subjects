# 10-10 第二篇

**E-Waste: What Happens to Our Old Electronics?**

When we get a new phone or laptop, we often forget about the old one. Yet billions of electronic devices are thrown away each year—phones, computers, TVs, and chargers. This growing pile of "e-waste" is a serious problem for our health and the environment. [1] Old electronics contain harmful chemicals such as lead and mercury, which can leak into soil and water if not handled properly, and they also waste valuable materials like gold and copper that could be recycled and reused.

Many people do not know what to do with broken or outdated devices. Some throw them in the trash. Others store them in drawers forever. Few realize that e-waste can and should be recycled. [2] Many stores and manufacturers now offer take-back programs, and some cities have special collection days or drop-off sites where people can bring their old electronics for safe disposal or recycling.

Recycling e-waste is not simple. Workers must carefully take apart devices to separate metals, plastics, and other parts. In some poorer countries, people do this work without proper protection, breathing in toxic fumes and touching dangerous chemicals. [3] Better laws, safer technology, and support for recycling centers that treat workers fairly can reduce these harms and make e-waste recycling safer for everyone involved.

As consumers, we can slow the flow of e-waste. We can repair devices instead of replacing them, donate working items to schools or charities, and choose products that are designed to last longer. [4] Thinking before we buy—do we really need an upgrade?—can also cut down on unnecessary e-waste and save money as well as resources.

E-waste will continue to grow unless we change how we use and discard electronics. Governments, companies, and citizens all have a role to play. [5] By reducing what we buy, reusing what we have, and recycling what we no longer need, we can turn the e-waste problem into an opportunity to protect the planet and build a more sustainable future.

## 选项

A. If e-waste is dumped in landfills or burned, it pollutes the environment and endangers both wildlife and human health, while the valuable materials inside go to waste instead of being recovered.

C. Improving conditions for workers and investing in cleaner, more efficient recycling methods is essential if we want e-waste recycling to be both effective and ethical.
D. Small changes in how we buy, use, and dispose of electronics can add up to a big difference—and they often save us money and protect our data as well.
E. A circular approach—where old devices become resources for new ones—is possible only when everyone does their part, from designers who make products easier to recycle to consumers who choose to recycle.
F. For example, a single smartphone can contain more than 60 different elements, including rare metals that are difficult to mine and are in high demand for new devices.
