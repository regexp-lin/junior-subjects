**Desertification: When Land Turns to Sand**

Desertification is the process by which fertile land becomes desert. It happens when soil loses its ability to support plants and crops. Climate change, over farming, deforestation, and poor water management all contribute to this problem. [1] Once land becomes degraded, it is very hard to restore, and millions of people who depend on farming and herding lose their livelihoods as the soil turns to dust.

The causes of desertification are often linked to human activity. When too many animals graze on the same land, they eat the grass down to the roots. When trees are cut for fuel or farmland, the soil loses its protective cover and is blown or washed away by wind and rain. [2] In dry regions, using too much water for crops or letting irrigation systems fall apart can drain underground water and leave the soil too salty or dry for plants to grow.

Desertification affects not only the land but also the people who live on it. As crops fail and water becomes scarce, many families are forced to leave their homes and move to cities or other regions in search of work and food. [3] This movement of people can strain resources in new areas and lead to conflict over land and water, yet it also highlights the urgent need for solutions that help people stay on their land and adapt to changing conditions.

There are ways to fight desertification. Planting trees and grasses, rotating crops, and using water more wisely can help protect and restore soil. In some places, farmers have built terraces or used techniques that trap rainwater in the soil. [4] Success often depends on local knowledge, support from governments and organizations, and the willingness of communities to work together over many years.

Stopping desertification requires action at every level—from individual farmers to international agreements. World leaders have pledged to combat land degradation and to support communities that are most at risk. [5] With better practices, stronger policies, and continued research, we can slow the spread of deserts and give the land—and the people who depend on it—a chance to recover and thrive again.

## 选项

A. The damage can last for generations, and families who once grew food and raised animals find themselves with nothing to live on as the land slowly turns to sand.
B. Understanding these causes is the first step toward finding solutions—and many solutions start with better land use, smarter water management, and protecting existing vegetation.
C. The social and economic impacts go beyond the immediate area: migration, poverty, and competition for resources can spread to nearby regions and even across borders.
D. Restoring degraded land is possible, but it takes time, money, and cooperation—and it works best when local people are involved in planning and carrying out the work.
E. Reversing desertification is a long-term task, but with the right tools, policies, and commitment from governments and communities, progress is possible and hope remains.
F. For example, the Sahel region in Africa has seen both severe desertification and successful projects where communities planted millions of trees to bring back vegetation and hold the soil in place.

## 答案键（Answer Key）

[1] A   [2] B   [3] C   [4] D   [5] E

- **[1] A**：
  - **线索**：空前说沙漠化概念及成因，空后说"Once land becomes degraded, it is very hard to restore, and millions of people who depend on farming and herding lose their livelihoods"。A的"The damage can last for generations... families who once grew food... find themselves with nothing to live on as the land slowly turns to sand"与空后难以恢复、民众失所呼应。
