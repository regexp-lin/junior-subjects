**Air Quality: Breathing Cleaner Air**

The air we breathe affects our health every day. In many cities around the world, air pollution has become a serious problem. Vehicle exhaust, factory emissions, and burning coal or wood release tiny particles and harmful gases into the air. When pollution levels are high, people may experience coughing, eye irritation, and difficulty breathing. Long-term exposure has been linked to heart disease, lung problems, and other health issues. [1]

Air quality is often measured by the Air Quality Index, or AQI. The index uses numbers and colors to show how clean or polluted the air is. Green usually means good, while orange, red, or purple indicate worsening conditions. Many weather apps and websites now display AQI so people can plan outdoor activities on cleaner days. On bad air days, health experts recommend staying indoors, closing windows, and avoiding heavy exercise outside. [2]

Improving air quality requires changes in how we produce energy and travel. Shifting from fossil fuels to renewable sources like solar and wind reduces emissions. Electric vehicles and better public transport can cut pollution from cars. Cities can plant more trees, which absorb carbon dioxide and filter pollutants. Some countries have set strict rules for factories and vehicles, and air quality has improved as a result. [3]

Individuals can also contribute. Using less energy at home, choosing walking or biking for short trips, and avoiding burning trash or leaves helps. Supporting policies that promote clean air and holding leaders accountable can drive bigger changes. Air quality is a shared resource—what one person or country does affects others nearby. [4]

Clean air is a right and a responsibility. By understanding the sources of pollution and taking action at every level, we can work toward a future where everyone can breathe easier. [5]
