**Microplastics: A Hidden Danger in Our Environment**

When plastic bags and bottles break down in the environment, they do not disappear completely. They break into smaller and smaller pieces, some so tiny that we can hardly see them. These tiny pieces are called microplastics. [1] In fact, microplastics are plastic particles smaller than five millimetres—about the size of a sesame seed（芝麻）or smaller. They can come from large plastic waste that breaks apart, from microbeads in some face washes and toothpastes, or from synthetic（合成的）clothes when we wash them. That is why microplastics are now found almost everywhere: in the ocean, in soil, in the air we breathe, and even in our drinking water.

Where do microplastics go? [2] They are so small that they can enter the food chain easily. Small sea animals eat them, thinking they are food. Bigger fish eat those small animals, and the microplastics stay in their bodies. In the end, when we eat seafood, we may be eating microplastics too. In other words, microplastics move from the environment into animals and then into us. Scientists are still studying how much harm they cause to human health, but the presence of microplastics in our food and water is a growing concern.

Why are they hard to remove? [3] Because microplastics are so tiny, they pass through most filters and treatment systems. They spread through wind and water to places far from where they were first released. Once in the environment, they can stay for hundreds of years. Cleaning them from the ocean or soil is extremely difficult and expensive. For this reason, the best way to deal with microplastics is to reduce plastic use at the source—use less plastic, so fewer microplastics are created.

What can we do? [4] We can avoid products that contain microbeads and choose natural alternatives. Washing synthetic clothes less often or using a laundry bag that catches microfibres can help. Choosing products with less packaging and refusing single-use plastics also reduces the amount of plastic that may eventually become microplastics. So every small change in our daily habits can help slow down the growth of microplastic pollution.

[5] In short, microplastics are a serious environmental problem that affects both wildlife and ourselves. Although we cannot solve it overnight, we can take steps to reduce our plastic footprint. By being more conscious of what we buy and how we dispose of waste, we protect not only the planet but also our own health. Remember: the less plastic we use today, the fewer microplastics our children will face tomorrow.
