**School Volunteering: Learning by Helping Others**

Many schools encourage students to take part in volunteering activities. Instead of only studying in the classroom, students go out to help their community—visiting the elderly, cleaning up parks, tutoring younger children, or raising money for those in need. [1] In fact, volunteering is not just about giving; it is also about learning. When students help others, they develop new skills, meet people from different backgrounds, and see the world from a new angle. They learn that their actions can make a real difference. That is why volunteering is increasingly seen as an important part of education, not just an extra activity.

What skills do students gain from volunteering? [2] They learn to work in teams, communicate with different people, and solve problems in real situations. Organising a charity run or a food drive requires planning, teamwork, and persistence. Students may also discover interests they never knew they had—for example, someone who helps at an animal shelter might decide to become a vet（兽医）one day. In other words, volunteering helps students grow both as individuals and as members of society. The experience often stays with them long after they leave school.

Volunteering also builds empathy and responsibility. [3] When students spend time with elderly people who live alone or with children who need extra help, they begin to understand the challenges others face. This can make them more grateful for what they have and more willing to help in the future. Many students say that volunteering changed how they think about their community and their role in it. For this reason, schools that offer volunteering programmes are not only teaching kindness but also shaping future citizens who care about others.

How can schools support volunteering? [4] They can partner with local charities, nursing homes, or environmental groups to find suitable projects. They can set aside time during the school year for volunteering—for example, a "service day" when the whole school takes part. Teachers can help students reflect on what they learned and how they felt after each activity. So with the right support, volunteering becomes a meaningful part of school life rather than something students do only to fulfil a requirement.

[5] In short, school volunteering benefits both the community and the students themselves. It teaches skills that cannot be learned from textbooks alone, builds character, and creates a habit of giving back. Whether it is an hour a week or a project during the holidays, every act of volunteering counts. Remember: when young people learn to care for others, they build a better world for everyone. So next time your school announces a volunteering opportunity, consider joining in—you might surprise yourself with what you can achieve.
