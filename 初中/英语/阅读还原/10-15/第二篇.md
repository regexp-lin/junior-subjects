# 10-15 第二篇

**Polar Exploration: Journey to the Ends of the Earth**

The Arctic and Antarctica are among the coldest, most remote places on Earth. For centuries, explorers risked their lives to reach these frozen landscapes. Today, scientists continue to travel there to study climate, wildlife, and ice. Polar exploration has taught us much about our planet—and it continues to reveal how the polar regions are changing as the world warms. [1] The polar regions hold clues to Earth's past climate, support unique wildlife such as polar bears and penguins, and play a vital role in regulating global temperatures and sea levels.

Early polar exploration was extremely dangerous. Ships could get stuck in ice. Explorers faced frostbite, hunger, and months of darkness in winter. Some expeditions, such as those of Scott and Amundsen racing to the South Pole, became famous for both their courage and their tragedies. [2] Despite the risks, each expedition brought back new knowledge about geography, weather, and survival in the harshest conditions on Earth, and this knowledge paved the way for modern science and travel in the polar regions.

Today, polar research focuses on understanding climate change. Scientists drill into ice cores to see how the climate has changed over thousands of years. They study how melting ice affects ocean currents and sea levels. They observe how polar bears, seals, and penguins are adapting—or struggling—as their habitat changes. [3] This research is essential because the polar regions are warming faster than the rest of the planet, and what happens there affects weather patterns, coastlines, and ecosystems worldwide.

Protecting the polar regions has become a global priority. Many countries have agreed to limit fishing, reduce pollution, and protect wildlife in parts of the Arctic and Antarctic. Tourists who visit must follow strict rules to avoid disturbing animals and damaging the environment. [4] Balancing exploration, science, and tourism with protection of these fragile ecosystems is a challenge that requires cooperation among nations and respect for the limits of these unique places.

The story of polar exploration is still being written. As ice melts and new shipping routes open, the Arctic is becoming more accessible—and more at risk. [5] The future of the polar regions will depend on how we balance our curiosity and our need for resources with the responsibility to protect these extraordinary parts of our planet for generations to come.

## 选项

A. The Arctic and Antarctic are not only important for science—they influence global climate, house species found nowhere else, and help us understand how Earth's systems work and how they are changing.
B. The hardships of early explorers were not in vain—their maps, diaries, and discoveries laid the foundation for the scientific work that continues today in both the Arctic and Antarctica.
C. Understanding the polar regions is urgent—they are changing rapidly, and the data collected there helps scientists predict how climate change will affect the rest of the world, from sea level rise to extreme weather.
D. Preserving these regions requires careful management—international agreements, responsible tourism, and a commitment to put the health of these ecosystems ahead of short-term economic gains.
E. How we treat the polar regions in the coming decades will shape not only their future but also ours—and the choices we make now will determine whether these frozen wonders remain for future generations to study and admire.
F. For example, Ernest Shackleton's expedition to Antarctica in 1914 became famous when his ship was crushed by ice, and he led his crew on an incredible journey to safety across frozen seas and mountains.

## 答案键（Answer Key）

[1] A   [2] B   [3] C   [4] D   [5] E
