# 08-24 第二篇

**Learning to Plan Your Time**

Time is the same for everyone—twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week. Yet some people achieve much more than others. The difference often lies in how they use their time. Students who struggle with deadlines, unfinished homework, and last-minute cramming usually have one thing in common: they have not learned to plan. [1] Time management is a skill that can be learned, and it can transform how you study and live. The earlier you start, the more benefits you will see.

The first step is to know where your time goes. Many students are surprised when they track their daily activities. They may spend hours on social media or video games without realizing it. [2] Writing down what you do each day helps you see patterns and spot wasted time. Once you know the truth, you can make better choices. You might decide to limit screen time or set aside specific hours for study. Awareness is the foundation of change.

A useful tool is the to-do list. Write down all your tasks—homework, projects, chores, and activities. Then put them in order of importance and deadline. [3] Tackle the most important or urgent items first, and break big tasks into smaller steps. This keeps you from feeling overwhelmed and helps you make steady progress. Crossing off completed items gives you a sense of achievement and motivation to continue.

Saying no is part of good time management. You cannot do everything. [4] Learning to decline unnecessary invitations or activities protects your priorities. When friends want to hang out during your study block, or when a new hobby tempts you away from your goals, remind yourself what matters most. Protecting your time is not selfish—it is necessary for achieving what you want.

[5] Time planning is not about filling every minute with work. It is about creating balance: enough time for study, rest, friends, and fun. A well-planned schedule reduces stress and increases both productivity and happiness. Start with a simple weekly plan, adjust it as you go, and soon you will find that you have more control over your life than you ever thought possible.

B. Recording your activities reveals how you actually spend time and where you can improve.

## 答案键（Answer Key）

[1] A   [2] B   [3] C   [4] D   [5] E

## 深度解析

- **[1] A**：
  - **线索**：空前描述不善规划的学生面临的 deadline、未完成作业、临时抱佛脚等问题；空后强调“越早开始，受益越多”。A项“这一技能可以学习，并能改变你的学习与生活；尽早培养会大幅提升学业和日常状态”承上概括时间管理的价值，启下引出尽早开始的好处，衔接自然。

- **[2] B**：
  - **线索**：空前讲追踪日常活动会让很多人惊讶，发现大量时间花在社交媒体或游戏上；空后写“了解真相后可以做出更好选择，如限制屏幕时间或固定学习时段”。B项“记录你的活动能反映真实的时间使用，并指出可以改进之处”准确概括本段要点。

- **[3] C**：
  - **线索**：空前介绍待办清单、按重要性和截止日期排序；空后写“先处理最重要或最紧急的事项，把大任务拆成小步骤”。C项“给任务排优先级并拆成步骤，使大目标更易管理”概括方法及作用，与前后逻辑一致。

- **[4] D**：
  - **线索**：空前强调不可能做所有事；空后写学会拒绝不必要的邀请或活动、保护优先事项，举例朋友邀约或新爱好干扰学习。D项“拒绝非必要请求有助于专注于真正重要的事”概括“说不”的意义。

- **[5] E**：
  - **线索**：空前为第四段；空后解释时间规划不是塞满每一分钟，而是创造平衡，并提到周密计划能减轻压力、提高效率与幸福感。E项“有效的时间管理带来平衡、压力减轻和更强的掌控感”作为结尾主题句，总结全文。

- **干扰项分析**：
  - **F. For example, some students use apps on their phones to set reminders for homework deadlines.**：以For example开头，为使用 app 提醒的具体例子。虽与时间规划相关，但属工具层面细节，无法承担[1]–[5]的概括或递进功能；易误选为[2]或[3]。
