# 04-11 第二篇

**The Art of a Good Apology**

We all make mistakes—saying something unkind, forgetting a promise, or hurting someone's feelings. When we do, a simple "sorry" is often not enough. [1] A real apology is more than just a word; it is a clear way to show that you understand what went wrong and that you care about repairing the relationship. Learning to apologize well can help you keep friendships strong and build trust with others.

What makes an apology effective? First, you need to take responsibility. [2] Instead of saying "I'm sorry if you felt hurt," say "I'm sorry that I said those words—they were wrong." When you use "if," it can sound like you are not sure you did anything wrong. Taking full responsibility shows the other person that you are serious about making things right. It also shows that you are willing to admit your mistake without excuses.

Second, show that you understand the impact of your actions. [3] You might say something like, "I know that what I did made you feel ignored, and I regret that." This lets the person know that you have thought about how your behavior affected them. It is not enough to say "I'm sorry" and move on—you need to show that you really see things from their point of view. Empathy makes an apology feel genuine.

Third, offer to make amends when possible. [4] If you broke a promise, you could offer to make up for it in a specific way. If you hurt someone's feelings, you could ask what you can do to help them feel better. Sometimes a simple promise to try not to repeat the mistake is enough. The key is to show that you are committed to change, not just saying empty words.

[5] A good apology can turn a moment of conflict into a chance to grow closer. It takes courage to admit when we are wrong, but the result—a restored relationship—is worth it. Practice these steps the next time you need to say you are sorry, and you may find that your connections with others become stronger.
