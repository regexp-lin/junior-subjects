# 06-04 第一篇

**The Evolution of the Piano**

The piano is one of the most popular instruments in the world. From concert halls to school classrooms, its beautiful sound has touched millions of people. [1] But few people know that the piano has a rich history of over 300 years, and it has changed greatly since it was first invented. Understanding this history helps us appreciate not only the music it makes, but also the creativity and skill of the people who built it.

The first piano was invented around 1700 by Bartolomeo Cristofori in Italy. Before the piano, people played the harpsichord（羽管键琴）, which could not play loud or soft—every note had the same volume. [2] Cristofori wanted to create an instrument that allowed musicians to control the dynamics, or how loud and soft each note could be. He called his invention "pianoforte," which means "soft-loud" in Italian. This new ability to play with different dynamics changed music forever and made the piano much more expressive than earlier keyboards.

Over the next two centuries, piano makers improved the instrument in many ways. [3] They made the frame stronger so it could hold thicker strings, which produced a richer and louder sound. They added more keys—early pianos had about 60 keys, while modern pianos have 88. The pedals we use today to sustain or soften notes were also developed during this time. Each improvement made the piano more powerful and more suitable for large concert halls.

In the 20th century, new technologies brought more changes. [4] The electric piano and later the digital piano allowed people to play with headphones, practice silently, or perform in places where a large acoustic piano would not fit. Today, you can find pianos in almost every style—from grand pianos in theatres to small electronic keyboards in homes. The piano has adapted to modern life while keeping its place as a beloved instrument.

[5] The piano's journey from a simple idea to a global icon shows how innovation can shape art and culture. It has inspired composers, performers, and learners for generations. Whether you play on an old wooden piano or a modern digital one, you are part of a long and beautiful tradition that continues to grow.
