**First Aid: Skills That Can Save Lives**

Accidents and emergencies can happen anywhere—at home, at school, or on the playground. When someone is hurt or suddenly falls ill, the first few minutes often matter most. Knowing basic first aid can help you stay calm, take the right actions, and possibly save a life before professional help arrives. [1] Learning how to stop bleeding, perform CPR, and treat burns or choking can make the difference between life and death in an emergency, and these skills are useful for people of all ages.

The first step in any emergency is to stay calm and assess the situation. Check if the scene is safe for you to approach. Then see if the person is conscious and breathing. Call for help—dial the emergency number—and stay on the line until help arrives. [2] While waiting for professionals, you can provide basic care such as applying pressure to a wound to stop bleeding, or keeping an injured person still if you suspect a broken bone, which can prevent further harm.

For someone who is choking, quick action is crucial. If a person cannot cough, speak, or breathe, the Heimlich maneuver—pushing firmly on the abdomen—can dislodge the object blocking the airway. For someone who has stopped breathing, CPR can keep blood flowing to the brain until paramedics arrive. [3] These techniques are simple to learn in a first aid class, and anyone can be trained to use them correctly—even teenagers can help save a family member or friend in an emergency.

Small injuries are common too. For cuts and scrapes, cleaning the wound and covering it with a clean bandage can prevent infection. For minor burns, cooling the area with cool (not cold) running water for several minutes can reduce pain and damage. [4] Knowing what to do for everyday injuries reduces panic, speeds healing, and can prevent a small problem from becoming a serious one.

First aid training is widely available. Schools, community centers, and organizations like the Red Cross offer courses for beginners. Many of these courses take just a few hours but can prepare you for a lifetime of helping others. [5] The more people who learn these skills, the safer our communities become—and you never know when you might be the one person who can make a difference when it matters most.
